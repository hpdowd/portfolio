---
title: "Green every night"
description: "A nightly database backup that reported success for weeks while quietly doing the one thing it existed to avoid, and the GitOps reconciler that was undoing it seconds after it happened."
date: 2026-06-15
---

For weeks the Gitea backup went green every single night, and that, it turns out, is exactly why the problem lived as long as it did. The job did what it was supposed to on paper. It scaled Gitea down to zero so that nothing was writing, copied the now-idle SQLite database off to the backup repository, and scaled it back up to one, logging a tidy "scaled down" and a clean exit every time. The snapshots were there, they restored, the integrity check passed on a test restore, and every dashboard I owned was the colour I wanted it to be. So there was nothing at all, no alert and no failure and no symptom, pointing at the fact that the single guarantee the whole dance existed to provide was not actually being provided.

It only gave itself away because I happened to watch a manual run live rather than trust the log. I kicked the backup off by hand and kept an eye on the running Gitea pod, and the thing that stopped me was its age. The pod had been recreated seconds into the backup window, which is the exact opposite of what "scaled to zero" is meant to mean. So I started polling the replica count directly, every ten seconds through the run, and I never once caught it at zero even as the job cheerfully logged that it had scaled things down. Something was putting Gitea back up faster than I could see it, and the job never noticed because its own scale-back-up step still ran and it still exited clean.

The culprit was the very thing that makes the cluster trustworthy the rest of the time. ArgoCD runs with self-heal on, which means it treats any live difference from what is committed in git as drift to be corrected, and it corrects it within seconds. And the backup's scale-to-zero is, as far as ArgoCD is concerned, precisely that kind of drift, because the committed manifest plainly says Gitea should have one replica. So the nightly sequence was never scale-down, copy, scale-up at all. It was scale-down, ArgoCD immediately putting it straight back up, and then the copy running against a live, mid-write database, with the job's own scale-back-up a no-op after the fact. The honest way to answer "who keeps changing this field" turned out to be the managed-fields view, which named the ArgoCD controller as the writer of the replica count at the very second the pod came back. It settled in two lines what log-reading had been going in circles on.

There was a small and almost funny trap folded into fixing it, which is that my first fix looked like it had failed. I told ArgoCD to stop caring about that deployment's replica count, pushed the change, re-ran the test inside the minute, and watched the race happen all over again. The fix was correct. It simply was not live yet, because the ArgoCD object that manages Gitea is itself managed by another one on a slower poll, so the cluster had not been told about my change at the moment I was testing it. The lesson there is one I have relearned more than once: when you are testing a GitOps fix, confirm the spec is actually live in the cluster before you conclude anything, not just that it is committed. The fix itself is two settings that only work together, one telling ArgoCD to ignore differences on the replica field and the other telling it to honour that rule during a sync and not only during a diff. With both in place I finally watched the count sit genuinely at zero for a real stretch of the backup, the new pod come up strictly after the copy had finished, and the app stay in sync the whole time, and only then was the guarantee the backup had always claimed actually true.

Nothing was ever corrupted by this, because Gitea at half three in the morning is idle and its database had checkpointed cleanly, so I got lucky in the way you only ever find out about after the fact. But luck is not the property you want a backup to have, and not depending on it was the entire reason the scale-down existed. The thing I took away is larger than one job. Any automation that reaches in and changes a GitOps-managed workload is quietly fighting the reconciler unless you tell the reconciler to stand back. And a green checkmark that certifies a step *ran* is a very different thing from one that certifies the step did what it was for.
