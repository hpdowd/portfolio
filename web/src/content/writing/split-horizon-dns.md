---
title: "Split-horizon, and a router that eats port 53"
description: "A wildcard certificate that would not issue because a name inside my network resolves differently than it does outside it, wrapped around a consumer router that silently drops every DNS query to the outside world."
date: 2026-06-20
---

The first time I stood up cert-manager to get a proper wildcard certificate for the LAN, it simply would not issue, and the way it failed sent me down through two apparently separate problems that turned out to be the same problem wearing two coats. The certificate sat there pending. The challenge records had been created correctly out in Cloudflare, the API token worked, the account was registered, everything up to the final step was green. And yet cert-manager would not proceed, because before it asks the certificate authority to validate a challenge it runs its own self-check first, and that self-check was failing to look up a record it had itself just written.

The first error named a hostname I did not expect. It was trying to dial the bare name of my own internal resolver, which is not any public nameserver at all. This is split-horizon DNS doing exactly what I built it to do and exactly what I did not want here. The same domain resolves one way inside the house, pointed at my own resolver so LAN traffic stays local, and another way out on the public internet. cert-manager, running inside the cluster, resolves the domain's authoritative nameserver the only way the cluster knows how, which is through that internal resolver. And the internal resolver answers with itself, a bare local name that resolves nowhere from inside the cluster. So the self-check loops forever, and even if it had resolved, it would have been interrogating the wrong server entirely, because the record it needs lives out in Cloudflare and never in my local zone.

The obvious next move was to tell the self-check to skip my resolver and ask a public one directly, and that is where the second coat came off. Now, instead of resolving the wrong answer, it timed out getting no answer at all. I tested it straight from my own workstation on the same network, querying a couple of the well-known public resolvers over both UDP and plain TCP, and every single one of them timed out. The consumer router this whole homelab sits behind silently drops outbound port 53 to the outside world, it does not block it with an error, it just quietly eats it. My own resolver only works at all because it forwards its upstream queries over an encrypted transport rather than over plain port 53, which is the one way out the router does not interfere with. And there was a small, unpleasant bonus buried in this. A document-editing feature that had been leaning on exactly the port the router eats had been silently broken for who knows how long, with nothing anywhere alerting on it.

The fix, once both coats were off, is almost tidy. Point the self-check at public resolvers but reach them over DNS-over-HTTPS, which rides ordinary port 443 straight through the router that mangles 53 and hands back the public view of the record without ever touching my local zone. The certificate issued within the minute. What I actually keep from this is a rule about the network rather than about cert-manager, plain DNS to public resolvers is dead from anywhere on this LAN, so anything in here that genuinely needs the outside world's view of a name has to go over an encrypted transport or through the resolver that already does. And the deeper, more uncomfortable half of it is that both failures were silent in the same way. The certificate at least refused to issue and so announced itself, but the broken document editing just stopped working and sat there waiting to be noticed, which is the kind of failure a self-hosted setup produces most easily and catches least.
