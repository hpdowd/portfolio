---
title: "Detected Hardware Unit Hang"
description: "An overnight outage that turned out to be the onboard NIC wedging itself, the ten-second log check that would have found it straight away, and the three-layer fix I deliberately chose never to trim down."
date: 2026-07-09
---

The whole homelab went dark overnight, and not in any of the ways I expected it to. I could not reach the Proxmox web interface, I could not SSH in from my laptop or my phone or anything else on the network. The machine sat there under the desk apparently dead to the world until I gave up and held the power button in to force it off. The one assumption I made and should not have was that the box was dead. It was not. Everything above the network had been running the whole time, the VMs, the cluster, the lot, and what had actually died was the single onboard network card that all of it reaches the world through. A wedged NIC on a headless server is indistinguishable from a dead server right up until the moment you put a monitor on it.

My first instinct was the wrong one, and it is worth writing down that it was wrong. I had deployed the monitoring stack earlier that same day, the host had been sitting close to its memory ceiling with swap already in use, and so the tidy story wrote itself: something had run the box out of RAM in the night. It was a neat theory and it was completely false. There was no out-of-memory activity anywhere in the journal, the ZFS ARC was already capped well below the ceiling, and the memory pressure I had been so suspicious of had nothing whatsoever to do with it. What actually settled it was the most boring move available, pulling the previous boot's kernel log with `journalctl -b -1 -e`. The answer was sitting right there at the end of it, one line repeating every two seconds until the moment I had cut the power: "Detected Hardware Unit Hang". The correct cause was visible in about ten seconds, and I had spent a great deal longer than ten seconds chasing a memory problem that did not exist. So the lesson I took from the night is smaller and far more useful than the fix itself, read the last boot's log before you form a single theory, because the machine has very often already told you exactly what happened and is only waiting for you to look.

The card is an Intel I219-LM, the onboard gigabit NIC on a great many small Intel desktops, and the hang is a known and long-lived bug in its e1000e driver. The transmit ring stalls, the driver notices and cannot recover it, and it settles into logging that same hang line every two seconds while the interface stays silent until the host is rebooted. It is aggravated by exactly the features that are meant to make the card faster and greener, the hardware offloads that let it chop up packets itself and the power-management tricks that let the link and the PCIe bus idle down between frames. So the fix is less a repair than a deliberate stripping-out of cleverness the card cannot be trusted to perform. I applied it in three layers, and the fact that it is three and not one turns out to be the whole point of the story. First the segmentation and receive offloads and the "energy efficient ethernet" idle come off, held down across reboots by a small systemd one-shot; then the driver is told to throttle its interrupts conservatively through a module parameter; and last, active-state power management on the PCIe bus is disabled at the kernel command line. Two of the three carried a quiet trap that cost me a second evening, because "EEE=0" looks exactly like it should be a valid module option and simply is not one for this driver, so that setting has to come off through ethtool instead. The interrupt-throttle parameter, meanwhile, does nothing at all until you rebuild the initramfs, which I had not done the first time and so had spent a while convinced a fix was live when it was doing nothing.

There was one more trap after that, and this one was purely me. Right after the fix and the reboot the card looked as though it had hung again inside the first minute, and my heart sank. It had not. What I was watching was the ordinary window at startup where Proxmox has brought the physical interface up but not yet the bridge that sits on top of it, a brief and entirely expected gap that looks precisely like a failure if you have been primed to see one. And the tell is simple once you are calm about it: a real hang is a repeating kernel error, and this was only silence that resolved itself the instant the bridge came up. The lesson there is nearly the twin of the first, wait for the whole boot to finish before you decide a fix has failed.

So which of the three layers is the one actually holding the card together? I do not know, and I made a deliberate decision not to find out. Isolating it would mean pulling the mitigations off one at a time and soaking each configuration for a week or more before I could trust it. And every one of those weeks would run on the only uplink the entire homelab has, deliberately re-exposing a single point of failure to a bug whose one and only recovery is me walking over and physically holding the power button in. Set against that, the cost of simply leaving all three in place is essentially nothing. The link is nowhere near saturated, so the lost offloads do not matter and power savings are meaningless on a machine that never sleeps anyway. So all three stay, indefinitely, and the honest engineering call is that a clean and satisfying root cause is not worth destabilising the one link everything else depends on in order to obtain it. If it ever hangs again with all three confirmed active then the software has run out of road, and the correct next move is not a fourth layer but a proper dedicated network card with this one disabled in the BIOS. What still nags at me more than the card itself is that all the while it was wedged I was completely blind. The very monitoring that is supposed to tell me why something is down runs on the same box that was down and is reachable only through the same NIC that had died, so the outage quietly erased its own evidence. A homelab that watches itself has this one irreducible hole in it, the thing that fails hardest is very often the thing that also carries the alarm, and the only real answer is a second small machine living outside the blast radius. That is the next thing to build.
