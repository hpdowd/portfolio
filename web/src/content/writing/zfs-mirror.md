---
title: "The redundancy I talked myself out of"
description: "Why I keep a two-disk ZFS mirror instead of splitting the disks and pushing redundancy up into the cluster's storage layer, on a box where that clever-sounding move would protect the wrong things."
date: 2026-07-06
---

Here is an idea I genuinely talked myself out of, and I am writing it down mostly so I do not sit and re-derive the whole argument the next time I catch myself eyeing that second two-terabyte disk. The temptation is to break the mirror. Right now my two spinning disks are a ZFS RAID-1 pair, which means I effectively have one disk's worth of space and a second identical copy of everything on it. The clever-sounding move is to split them into two independent single-disk pools, double the raw space, and push redundancy up a layer into the cluster's own storage instead, keeping two replicas only of the handful of things that truly must survive a disk dying and one replica of the bulk I would never have chosen to mirror anyway. In the abstract it is a good instinct. It is honestly how the big hyperconverged clusters run, plain disks underneath and the replication done by the orchestrator sitting on top. On this one box it is the wrong fit, for a stack of reasons that lean on one another.

The reason that weighed on me most is blast radius, because the mirror protects exactly the things the cluster's storage structurally cannot. That storage can only ever replicate volumes that live inside the cluster, and the two services I least want to lose, the DNS that every name on the network resolves through and the VPN that is my way back in when everything else is face-down, both live outside it on purpose. DNS cannot sensibly run inside the thing it has to resolve, and the VPN is the door I come home through when the cluster is a smoking hole. Those, and the virtual machines' own disks, all sit on the mirror today and are covered by it without my having to arrange anything. Split it into two single-disk pools and every one of them lands on one disk with no live redundancy at all. A single drive death now takes DNS down with it, which cascades straight into the cluster because every name resolves through that DNS, and it takes my remote way back in down at the very same moment. That moves protection off the pieces whose failure hurts most and is slowest to undo, and onto the application data that is already the best-backed-up thing I own. That is exactly backwards.

The rest of the reasons pile up behind that one. Memory is the constraint every other decision on this host bends around, and a second in-cluster replica roughly doubles the storage engine's footprint. So I would be spending the single scarcest thing I have to buy a weaker copy of redundancy I already get for free. These are ordinary hard disks with an extra network hop already sitting in front of the cluster's volumes. Putting every write across that hop to two disks and then through ZFS on each is a real and felt tax on spinning rust, where the mirror lays both copies down far more cheaply and much closer to the metal. A mirror also actively scrubs and repairs bit-rot out of the good copy, where a single-disk pool can still detect the corruption but has nothing to heal it from, so I would be handing back a property I get today for nothing. And the capacity win that started the whole daydream is mostly a mirage, because anything I keep at two replicas still costs me double, the very thing the mirror was already doing. The only genuine space is freed by plain-striping the two disks, which the cluster has nothing to do with, and nothing is short on space today anyway.

So the mirror stays, and redundancy stays down at the block layer where it covers the whole substrate at once rather than only the slice I remembered to enrol. I do want to be honest that this is a decision about this box at this size and not a law. A third drive would change it immediately, since then the clean move is obvious: keep the mirror for everything that matters and stand a single-disk pool beside it for the bulk that never wanted a second copy. A second node would change it too, because replicating across machines is the right tool the moment this stops being a one-box lab and can ride out a whole node dying in a way a single-box mirror never can. Short of either, the mirror is the floor and the nightly off-site backup is what catches everything the mirror cannot, theft, fire, or a fat-fingered destroy of the wrong dataset. And the one piece of homework any future rethink comes with is confirming the DNS and the VPN configs are genuinely in that off-site set first, because right now the mirror is the only thing covering them.
